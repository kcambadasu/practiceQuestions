The Framework6-Phase Financial Statement Analysis Process

1
Articulate Purpose & Context
Define why the analysis is being done. Who is the audience? What decisions will it support? Resist calculating ratios before understanding what's relevant.
→ Purpose statement + specific questions to answer
2
Collect Data
Gather financial statements, industry and economic data, management discussions. Top-down: macroeconomic → industry → company. Site visits and management meetings are valid data sources.
→ Financial data in usable form
3
Process Data
Compute ratios, growth rates, common-size statements. Adjust financial statements for comparability. Apply statistical analyses, regressions, and sensitivity analyses.
→ Adjusted statements, ratios, graphs
4
Analyze / Interpret Data
Numbers alone are never the answer. Interpret results in context to support a conclusion — buy/sell ratings, credit decisions, valuations.
→ Analytical results, forecasts, valuations
5
Communicate Conclusions & Recommendations
Report in the appropriate format for the audience. Equity reports include: investment conclusion, industry overview, financial model, valuation, and risks. CFA Standard V(B): distinguish opinions from facts.
→ Analytical report + recommendation
6
Follow-Up
Periodically repeat prior steps as new information arrives. Update forecasts and recommendations. Even rejected investments may require monitoring if conditions change.
→ Actual vs. expected comparison, revised forecasts

SEC FilingsKey Forms Analysts Use

10-K / 20-F / 40-F

Annual reports. 10-K for US companies, 20-F for non-US registrants, 40-F for certain Canadian registrants. Contains audited financials, MD&A, and risk factors.

10-Q / 6-K

Interim reports. 10-Q filed quarterly by US companies (unaudited). 6-K filed semi-annually by many non-US companies.

Form 8-K

Current report for material events — acquisitions, management changes, accounting changes, Reg FD disclosures. Must be filed promptly.

DEF-14A (Proxy)

Executive compensation, security ownership by management, director biographies, and shareholder vote items.

Forms 3, 4, 5 & 144

Insider ownership filings. Track beneficial ownership by directors, officers, and >10% holders. Form 144 covers proposed sale of restricted securities.

Registration Statement

Filed for new securities offerings. Includes risk factors, audited financial statements, use of proceeds, and capital structure.

✅

Audit ReportsFour Types of Opinion

Unqualified (Clean / Unmodified) — Preferred ✓

Financial statements are fairly presented in accordance with applicable accounting standards. Also called "true and fair view" internationally. This is the opinion analysts want to see.

Qualified — Some Exception or Scope Limitation

Fairly presented except for a specific issue. Exception described in additional explanatory paragraphs. Analyst must evaluate the importance of the exception.

Adverse — Material Departure from Standards ⚠

Financial statements materially depart from accounting standards and are not fairly presented. The most serious opinion — a major red flag.

Disclaimer — Unable to Issue an Opinion

Due to a scope limitation or other reason, the auditor is unable to express an opinion at all.

Audits use sampling techniques and statements involve estimates — auditors can only provide reasonable assurance, not absolute assurance. Always apply healthy scepticism even with a clean opinion.

Core Analyst Mindset

Financial statement analysis is not about verifying numbers — it's about forming expectations of future performance to support investment, credit, and other economic decisions. Combine quantitative outputs with qualitative interpretation, maintain healthy scepticism toward even audited figures, and continuously monitor changes in accounting standards that could affect comparability.

Rates and Returns

5 Learning Outcomes Interest Rate Components Return Measures MWR vs TWR Annualised Returns Leveraged Return
🎯

Learning OutcomesWhat You Need to Master

LO 1Interpret interest rates as required rates of return, discount rates, or opportunity costs and explain an interest rate as the sum of a real risk-free rate and risk premiums
LO 2Calculate and interpret different approaches to return measurement over time and describe their appropriate uses (arithmetic, geometric, harmonic, trimmed, winsorised means)
LO 3Compare the money-weighted and time-weighted rates of return and evaluate portfolio performance based on these measures
LO 4Calculate and interpret annualised return measures and continuously compounded returns, and describe their appropriate uses
LO 5Calculate and interpret major return measures (gross/net, pre-tax/after-tax, real, leveraged) and describe their appropriate uses
📐

Section 1 — LO 1Interest Rates: Three Interpretations

1
Required Rate of Return
The minimum return an investor must receive to accept an investment. Compensates for the time value of money and all relevant risks.
→ Threshold below which an investor rejects the investment
2
Discount Rate
The rate used to find the present value of a future cash flow. A 5.26% discount rate makes USD 10,000 in one year equivalent to USD 9,500 today.
→ "Interest rate" and "discount rate" are used almost interchangeably
3
Opportunity Cost
The value forgone by choosing one course of action over another. Spending USD 9,500 today forgoes earning 5.26% by saving instead.
→ Cost of not investing (current consumption)
🔢

Section 1 — LO 1Components of Interest Rates

The Formula: r = Real risk-free rate + Inflation premium + Default risk premium + Liquidity premium + Maturity premium

Real Risk-Free Rate

Single-period rate for a completely risk-free security with no expected inflation. Reflects individuals' time preferences for current vs. future consumption. Varies continuously with economic conditions.

Inflation Premium

Compensates for the expected average inflation rate over the maturity of the debt. Inflation erodes purchasing power. Nominal risk-free rate ≈ Real risk-free rate + Inflation premium.

Default Risk Premium

Compensates investors for the possibility that the borrower fails to make promised payments. Higher for lower-quality issuers; zero for government T-bills (assumed risk-free).

Liquidity Premium

Compensates for the risk of loss if the investment must be sold quickly at below fair value. US T-bills have no liquidity premium; thinly traded bonds of small issuers carry significant liquidity premiums.

Maturity Premium

Compensates for increased sensitivity of bond price to interest rate changes as maturity extends. Longer-maturity government bonds typically carry a positive maturity premium over short-term bonds.

Key relationship: (1 + nominal risk-free rate) = (1 + real risk-free rate) × (1 + inflation premium). In practice approximated as: Nominal ≈ Real + Inflation premium.
📊

Section 2 — LO 2Which Mean to Use? Decision Guide

Q1
Is there compounding involved?
Yes → Use Geometric Mean. It captures how returns compound over time and reflects the actual terminal value of an investment.
Q2
Are you averaging rates or ratios (e.g. P/E)?
Yes → Use Harmonic Mean. Particularly appropriate for cost-averaging strategies where a fixed amount is invested at varying prices.
Q3
Are there extreme outliers you want to include but limit?
Yes → Use Winsorised or Trimmed Mean to reduce the influence of outliers. Trimmed removes them; winsorised replaces them with boundary values.
Q4
Single-period, symmetric data, no special concerns?
Yes → Use Arithmetic Mean. Simple, has well-known statistical properties, and is the best predictor of next-period single-period returns.
⚖️

General Annualisation Formula

R_annual = (1 + R_period)^c − 1, where c = number of compounding periods per year. For holding periods >1 year (e.g. 18 months): c = 2/3, so R_annual = (1 + R_18m)^(2/3) − 1.

Key Limitation of Annualisation

Implicit assumption that the sub-period return can be repeated for the entire year — often unrealistic for very short periods. A 5% weekly return annualises to 1,164% — meaningless as a forward-looking estimate.

Continuously Compounded Return

r_t,t+1 = ln(P_{t+1}/P_t) = ln(1 + R_{t,t+1}). Always less than the equivalent holding period return. Key property: multi-period continuously compounded returns ADD across sub-periods (vs. HPRs which multiply).

Continuously compounded formula: r₀,T = ln(P_T / P₀). Multi-period: r₀,T = r_{T-1,T} + r_{T-2,T-1} + … + r₀,₁ (additive property — very useful in models).
💼

Section 5 — LO 5Special Return Measures

📈 Gross Return

Return after deducting trading costs only. Excludes management fees, custody fees, taxes — as these vary by investor. Used to evaluate and compare manager skill on a like-for-like basis.

📉 Net Return

Gross return minus all managerial and administrative expenses. Represents what the investor actually earns. Small funds disadvantaged vs. large funds (fixed costs spread over smaller AUM).

🏛️ Pre-Tax Nominal

The default return measure — no adjustment for taxes or inflation. All return measures are pre-tax nominal unless otherwise stated.

🧾 After-Tax Nominal

Total return minus tax on dividends, interest, and realised gains. Tax treatment varies: long-term capital gains often preferential; interest taxed as ordinary income. Managers minimise taxes via security selection and low turnover.

📊 Real Return

(1 + real return) = (1 + nominal return) / (1 + inflation). Useful for comparing returns across time periods or across countries with different inflation rates. After-tax real return = what the investor truly receives.

🔧 Leveraged Return

R_L = R_P + (V_B / V_E) × (R_P − r_D). Leverage amplifies both gains and losses. Increases return only if R_P > r_D (portfolio return > borrowing cost). Created via borrowing or futures contracts.

🔢

Common PitfallsExam Traps to Avoid

❌ Arithmetic vs Geometric
  • Arithmetic mean is always ≥ geometric mean (except when all returns are equal)
  • Arithmetic is for forward-looking single-period estimates; Geometric is for multi-period historical rates
  • The more variable the returns, the greater the gap between arithmetic and geometric means
❌ MWR vs TWR Direction
  • If more capital is invested when returns are poor → MWR < TWR
  • If more capital is invested when returns are good → MWR > TWR
  • TWR is the CFA Institute-preferred measure for manager evaluation
❌ Gross vs Net Return
  • Trading expenses ARE deducted from gross return (they directly affect manager returns)
  • Management fees, custody fees, and taxes are NOT in gross return
  • Net return = gross return − management & administrative expenses
❌ Leverage Direction
  • Leverage amplifies BOTH gains AND losses (not just gains)
  • If R_P < r_D (portfolio return < borrowing cost), leverage decreases the return
  • If 50% is borrowed, gross return doubles — but interest cost must still be subtracted to get net

Core Mindset for Rates & Returns

Every return measure has a specific purpose. Geometric mean tells you what you actually earned by compounding. Arithmetic mean gives the best single-period forward estimate. Time-weighted return reveals manager skill free of client cash-flow noise. Real after-tax return is what the investor truly keeps. Always identify the purpose before choosing a measure — the CFA exam will test whether you know which metric is appropriate, not just whether you can calculate it.

Time Value of Money in Finance

3 Learning Outcomes Bond Pricing Equity Valuation Implied Return & Growth Cash Flow Additivity No-Arbitrage Pricing
🎯

Learning OutcomesWhat You Need to Master

LO 1Calculate and interpret the present value (PV) of fixed-income and equity instruments based on expected future cash flows
LO 2Calculate and interpret the implied return of fixed-income instruments and required return and implied growth of equity instruments given PV and cash flows
LO 3Explain the cash flow additivity principle, its importance for the no-arbitrage condition, and its use in calculating implied forward interest rates, forward exchange rates, and option values
📐

FoundationsCore TVM Relationships

FV = PV(1+r)ᵗ
Discrete compounding — standard formula
FV = PVeʳᵗ
Continuous compounding (t → ∞ periods)
PV = FV·(1+r)⁻ᵗ
Present value: discount FV back t periods
PV = FV·e⁻ʳᵗ
Continuous discounting equivalent
The discount rate r is always a per-period rate. For semiannual bonds: use r/2 as the periodic rate and 2N as the number of periods. Mixing annual rates with non-annual periods is a common exam error.
🏦

Section 1 — LO 1Fixed-Income Pricing: Three Cash Flow Patterns

1
Discount (Zero-Coupon) Bond
Investor pays PV today; receives a single principal (FV) at maturity. No intermediate cash flows. The spread (FV − PV) is the total interest earned.
→ PV = FVₜ / (1 + r)ᵗ  |  Excel: PV(r, t, 0, FV, 0)
2
Coupon Bond (Periodic Interest + Principal at Maturity)
Investor receives PMT at each period and FV at maturity. YTM is the single discount rate applied to all cash flows. When coupon rate = YTM → PV = FV (par). When YTM > coupon → PV < FV (discount). When YTM < coupon → PV > FV (premium).
→ PV = PMT/(1+r)¹ + … + (PMTₙ + FVₙ)/(1+r)ᴺ  |  Excel: PV(r, N, PMT, FV, 0)
3
Level-Payment / Annuity (Mortgage-Type)
Uniform periodic payment A covers both interest and principal repayment over the life of the loan. Interest portion declines each period as principal falls; principal portion rises. All 360 monthly payments are equal for a 30-year mortgage.
→ A = r·PV / [1 − (1+r)⁻ᵗ]  |  Excel: PMT(r, t, −PV, 0, 0)
Perpetual Bond: As N → ∞, coupon bond simplifies to PV = PMT / r. Used for bonds with no maturity date (e.g. preference shares, AT1 capital instruments). YTM = PMT / PV, so a price below par implies a higher yield.
🔢

Section 1 — LO 1Equity Valuation: Three Dividend Models

1
Constant Dividend (Zero Growth)
Stock pays a fixed dividend D every period forever (perpetuity). Same formula as a perpetual bond. Suitable for preferred shares or stable mature companies with fixed payouts.
→ PV = D / r
2
Constant Dividend Growth (Gordon Growth Model)
Dividend grows at constant rate g forever. Requires r > g. A higher growth rate g reduces the denominator (r − g), increasing the share price. The next dividend Dₜ₊₁ = Dₜ × (1 + g) is used (not the most recent dividend).
→ PV = Dₜ₊₁ / (r − g) = Dₜ(1+g) / (r − g)
3
Two-Stage (Changing) Dividend Growth
High initial growth gₛ for n periods, then slower long-term growth gₗ indefinitely. Step 1: PV of each dividend in high-growth stage. Step 2: PV of terminal value E(Sₙ) = Dₙ₊₁ / (r − gₗ) discounted back n periods. Step 3: Sum both.
→ PV = Σᵢ₌₁ⁿ [Dₜ(1+gₛ)ⁱ / (1+r)ⁱ] + E(Sₙ)/(1+r)ⁿ
Sensitivity check: As long-term growth gₗ increases (0% → 2% → 4% → 6%), the share price increases non-linearly because gₗ reduces the denominator in the terminal value formula. Small changes in gₗ near r have a large effect on PV.
🔍

Section 2 — LO 2Implied Return & Growth: Key Formulas

📉 Discount Bond Implied Return

When price and principal are known, solve for the annualised periodic rate.

r = (FV/PV)^(1/t) − 1

An investor who sells before maturity earns the implied return based on purchase price → sale price, not original YTM. If rates rise after purchase, implied return over the holding period is below YTM.

🔄 Coupon Bond YTM (Implied Return)

YTM is the IRR of all promised cash flows. Cannot be solved algebraically — use iteration (Excel RATE or YIELD function).

Assumes: (1) all cash flows received as promised, and (2) all coupon payments reinvested at the same YTM. The reinvestment rate assumption is critical — a key limitation of YTM.

📊 Equity Implied Return

Given current price and expected dividends + growth:

r = Dₜ₊₁/PVₜ + g

Implied return = expected dividend yield + constant growth rate. Simple interpretation: total return = income + capital appreciation.

🌱 Equity Implied Growth

Given current price, expected dividend, and required return:

g = r − Dₜ₊₁/PVₜ

Implied growth = required return minus expected dividend yield. If implied growth > analyst's estimate → stock may be overvalued. If implied growth < analyst's estimate → stock may be undervalued.

💹

Section 2 — LO 2Price-to-Earnings (P/E) Ratio & Valuation

Forward P/E formula: PVₜ / Eₜ₊₁ = (Dₜ₊₁/Eₜ₊₁) / (r − g) = Dividend payout ratio / (r − g)
P/E is higher when...P/E is lower when...
Growth rate g is higher (reduces denominator r − g)Required return r is higher (increases denominator)
Dividend payout ratio is higherDividend payout ratio is lower
Required return r is lowerGrowth rate g is lower

Overvalued Signal

Observed P/E > implied P/E (from fundamentals). The current price is too high relative to earnings, dividends, and growth expectations. Consider a short position or avoid.

Undervalued Signal

Observed P/E < implied P/E. Fundamentals (growth, payout, required return) justify a higher price than the market currently reflects. Consider a long position.

Fairly Valued

Observed P/E = implied P/E. Market price correctly reflects the combination of expected growth and required return embedded in the dividend payout ratio.

⚖️

Section 3 — LO 3Cash Flow Additivity & No-Arbitrage

Principle: The PV of any future cash flow stream equals the sum of the PVs of its individual cash flows (as long as they are indexed at the same point in time). Two strategies with identical cash flows must have the same price — otherwise a riskless arbitrage profit exists.
App 1
Implied Forward Interest Rates
A risk-neutral investor is indifferent between (1) investing at the 2-year spot rate for 2 years, or (2) investing at the 1-year spot rate and rolling over at the implied 1-year forward rate F₁,₁. No-arbitrage requires both strategies to yield the same FV.
→ F₁,₁ = (1+r₂)² / (1+r₁) − 1  |  F₁,₁ is the breakeven reinvestment rate in year 2
App 2
Forward Exchange Rates (Covered Interest Parity)
Investor is indifferent between (1) investing in domestic currency at domestic risk-free rate, or (2) converting to foreign currency, investing at foreign risk-free rate, and locking in a forward FX rate today. Both must yield the same domestic-currency return.
→ F₀ = S₀ × e^(r_d − r_f)T  |  If r_d > r_f, domestic currency depreciates on a forward basis
App 3
Option Pricing (Replicating Portfolio)
Construct a portfolio of the underlying asset and the option such that the portfolio's value is the same in all scenarios (risk-free). Discount this known future payoff at the risk-free rate to find the no-arbitrage option price today. The proportion of underlying asset held = hedge ratio.
→ PV(replicating portfolio) = V₁/(1+r)  |  Solve for c₀ or p₀
🔀

Section 3 — LO 3Arbitrage: How It Works & Why It Fails

📐 Forward Rate Arbitrage (Interest Rates)
  • If F₁,₁ is set too high: borrow for 2 years at r₂, invest year-by-year at r₁ then F₁,₁ → riskless profit
  • If F₁,₁ is set too low: the reverse strategy generates riskless profit
  • Markets force F₁,₁ to the exact breakeven level — F₁,₁ = (1+r₂)²/(1+r₁) − 1
  • In a rising-rate environment: F₁,₁ > r₂ > r₁ (upward-sloping yield curve)
💱 FX Forward Arbitrage (Interest Rate Parity)
  • If forward FX rate is too favourable: convert domestic → foreign, invest, convert back → riskless profit
  • Higher domestic interest rate → domestic currency depreciates on a forward basis
  • The forward rate is the only rate consistent with no-arbitrage given the two risk-free rates and spot exchange rate
📊 Call Option Replication
  • Call option: right to BUY at exercise price X; value = Max(0, S₁ − X) at expiry
  • Replicating portfolio: buy h units of stock + sell call → same payoff in both up/down scenarios
  • Hedge ratio h = (c_up − c_down) / (S_up − S_down)
  • PV of replicating portfolio = risk-free payoff discounted at risk-free rate → solves for c₀
📉 Put Option Replication
  • Put option: right to SELL at exercise price X; value = Max(0, X − S₁) at expiry
  • Replicating portfolio: buy h units of stock + buy put → same payoff in both scenarios
  • Put increases in value when stock falls (opposite to call)
  • Higher put payoff in the down scenario → higher put option price vs call under same parameters

  • Always halve the annual coupon AND the annual YTM for semiannual bonds
  • Double the number of periods (20-year semiannual = 40 periods)
  • Using 20 annual periods with semiannual rate gives a wrong (lower) PV
❌ Gordon Growth: Use Next Dividend

  • If given Dₜ (last paid), multiply by (1+g) to get Dₜ₊₁
  • Using last dividend instead of next dividend understates PV
❌ Forward Rate vs Spot Rate
  • F₁,₁ is not the expected future spot rate — it is the breakeven rate implied by current spot rates
  • In a rising-rate environment, F₁,₁ > r₂ > r₁
  • If F₁,₁ ≠ breakeven, an arbitrage opportunity exists
❌ YTM Reinvestment Assumption
  • YTM assumes coupon cash flows are reinvested at the same YTM — often unrealistic
  • If reinvestment rates are lower than YTM, actual return earned is less than YTM
  • This assumption is a key limitation of YTM as a return measure

Core Mindset for TVM in Finance

Every financial asset price is simply the present value of its expected future cash flows discounted at an appropriate risk-adjusted rate. The same TVM framework applies whether you're pricing a zero-coupon government bond, a dividend-growing stock, or a complex option — only the cash flow pattern and the discount rate change. Cash flow additivity ties everything together: identical future cash flows must have identical prices today, or a riskless arbitrage profit is available. Mastering the link between price, cash flows, and discount rate — and being able to solve for any one given the other two — is the single most tested skill in this reading.

Section 1 — LO 1Measures of Central Tendency

1
Arithmetic Mean ( X̄ = Σ Xᵢ / n )
Sum of all observations divided by the number of observations. Most common measure; used as default. Sensitive to outliers — a single extreme value pulls the mean significantly.
→ Best for symmetric distributions without extreme outliers
2
Median
Middle value when data are sorted. For odd n: value at position (n+1)/2. For even n: average of values at positions n/2 and (n+2)/2. Not affected by outliers — preferred for skewed distributions.
→ Preferred when outliers or skewness are present
3
Mode
Most frequently occurring value. A dataset can be unimodal (one mode), bimodal, trimodal, or have no mode. The only measure usable with nominal/categorical data.
→ Histogram bar with highest frequency = modal interval
4
Trimmed Mean
Arithmetic mean computed after removing a stated percentage of the extreme high and low values (e.g., 5% trimmed mean removes the bottom 2.5% and top 2.5% of values). Reduces the influence of outliers.
→ Used in sports scoring and robust return estimation
5
Winsorized Mean
Extreme values are replaced (not removed) by a specified percentile boundary. A 95% winsorized mean substitutes the 2.5th percentile value for all observations below it and the 97.5th percentile value for all above.
→ Preserves sample size; reduces distortion from extremes
Skewness and means: In a positively skewed distribution: Mode < Median < Mean. In a negatively skewed distribution: Mean < Median < Mode. In a symmetric distribution all three are equal.

Section 2 — LO 2Measures of Dispersion

Range

Maximum value − Minimum value. Simple but uses only two observations; highly sensitive to outliers. Limited informational value about the shape of the distribution.

Mean Absolute Deviation (MAD)

MAD = Σ|Xᵢ − X̄| / n. Average of the absolute deviations from the mean. Uses all observations. Technically harder to manipulate mathematically than variance.

Sample Variance (s²)

s² = Σ(Xᵢ − X̄)² / (n−1). Squared deviations from the mean, averaged using n−1 (degrees of freedom). The divisor n−1 makes s² an unbiased estimator of population variance. Units are squared.

Sample Standard Deviation (s)

s = √[Σ(Xᵢ − X̄)² / (n−1)]. Square root of the variance; same units as the data. Most widely used dispersion measure. Always reported alongside the arithmetic mean.

Target Downside Deviation (Semideviation)

S_Target = √[Σ(Xᵢ − B)² / (n−1)] for all Xᵢ ≤ B. Only counts observations below the target B. Higher target → more observations included → larger semideviation. Captures downside risk only.

Coefficient of Variation (CV)

CV = s / X̄. Risk per unit of mean return. Scale-free — allows comparison across datasets with different means or units. Higher CV = more risk per unit of reward. Meaningless if X̄ is negative.

Key relationship: For any distribution, Geometric Mean ≤ Arithmetic Mean. The greater the dispersion (higher variance), the larger the gap between them. This is why Portfolio B with higher standard deviation has a lower geometric mean than Portfolio A despite equal arithmetic means.

Section 4 — LO 4Correlation Between Two Variables

Correlation = +1 (Perfect Positive)

All scatter plot points lie on a positively sloped straight line. Every unit increase in X is associated with a fixed increase in Y. No diversification benefit.

Correlation near +1 (Strong Positive)

Variables move together most of the time. Tightly clustered scatter around an upward-sloping line. Example: IT sector vs. S&P 500 returns.

Correlation = 0 (Uncorrelated)

No linear relationship. Knowing X tells you nothing about Y. Points scatter randomly. Example: utilities sector vs. S&P 500 in some periods.

Correlation = −1 (Perfect Negative / Inverse)

All points on a negatively sloped straight line. Every increase in X is associated with a fixed decrease in Y. Maximum diversification benefit.

Key limitations of correlation:
  • Outliers — a single extreme observation can dramatically change the correlation coefficient.
  • Non-linear relationships — correlation measures only linear association; two variables perfectly associated in a non-linear way can have r ≈ 0.
  • Spurious correlation — high correlation may arise by chance, from dividing by a shared variable, or from a common third-variable driver. Correlation does not imply causation.
  • Anscombe's Quartet — four very different datasets can share identical means, standard deviations, and correlations. Always visualize data with a scatter plot before interpreting r.

Common PitfallsExam Traps to Avoid

❌ Variance Divisor: n vs n−1
  • Sample variance and standard deviation use n−1 (degrees of freedom), not n
  • Using n gives a biased (lower) estimate of population variance
  • Sample covariance also uses n−1 in the denominator
❌ Target Semideviation: Wrong n
  • Denominator is n−1 where n = total sample observations, not just observations below the target
  • Only the squared deviations are restricted to below-target observations in the numerator
❌ Skewness Signs and Tail Direction
  • Positive skew → right/upper tail is longer; mean > median > mode
  • Negative skew → left/lower tail is longer; mean < median < mode
  • Don't confuse skewness sign with which side investors prefer
❌ Excess Kurtosis vs Kurtosis
  • Normal distribution kurtosis = 3; excess kurtosis = 0
  • Statistical packages often report excess kurtosis labelled simply as "kurtosis"
  • Fat-tailed: excess kurtosis > 0; Thin-tailed: excess kurtosis < 0
❌ Correlation ≠ Causation
  • A high correlation does not imply one variable causes the other
  • Always suspect spurious correlation when the relationship has no logical basis
  • Correlation is only valid for linear relationships
❌ Percentile Location Formula
  • Use Lᵧ = (n+1) × (y/100) to find the position, then interpolate if non-integer
  • The 4th quintile = 80th percentile, NOT the top 20% group
  • Interpolation: Pᵧ = X_floor + (Lᵧ − floor) × (X_ceil − X_floor)

Core Mindset for Statistical Measures of Asset Returns

Return distributions almost never follow a perfect normal distribution. Real asset returns tend to be negatively skewed (catastrophic crashes are more likely than equivalent windfalls) and fat-tailed (extreme events occur more often than the normal curve predicts). This means that using only the mean and standard deviation to describe returns systematically underestimates tail risk. A complete picture requires examining skewness, kurtosis, downside semideviation, and visual tools like histograms and box plots. Similarly, when evaluating relationships between two assets, correlation summarizes only the linear part of the story — always supplement with a scatter plot and be alert to outliers and spurious correlations before drawing investment conclusions.

Section 1 — LO 1Expected Value, Variance & Standard Deviation

1
Expected Value — E(X)
The probability-weighted average of all possible outcomes of a random variable. It is a forward-looking forecast — not the same as a historical sample mean, which is an equally weighted average of past observations.
E(X) = P(X₁)X₁ + P(X₂)X₂ + … + P(Xₙ)Xₙ = Σ P(Xᵢ)Xᵢ
2
Variance — σ²(X)
The probability-weighted average of squared deviations from the expected value. Always ≥ 0. Variance = 0 means certainty (no risk). Variance is measured in squared units of X (e.g., percent²).
σ²(X) = Σ P(Xᵢ)[Xᵢ − E(X)]²
3
Standard Deviation — σ(X)
The positive square root of variance. Measured in the same units as X (e.g., percent), making it directly interpretable as a risk measure. Always reported alongside expected value.
σ(X) = √[Σ P(Xᵢ)(Xᵢ − E(X))²]
Calculation order is always: (1) Expected Value → (2) Variance → (3) Standard Deviation. You must have E(X) before you can compute variance.

Section 2 — LO 2Probability Trees & Conditional Expectations

1
Structure of a Probability Tree
A probability tree has a root node (starting point), branches leading to scenario nodes (e.g., high growth / low growth), and terminal nodes showing final outcomes with their probabilities. The probability of any terminal node = product of all probabilities along its branch path.
→ Terminal node probability = P(scenario) × P(outcome | scenario)
2
Conditional Expected Value
The expected value of X computed using only the probabilities and outcomes applicable within a specific scenario S, written E(X | S). Each scenario produces its own conditional expected value and conditional variance, enabling scenario-specific risk assessment.
→ E(X | S) = Σ P(Xᵢ | S) · Xᵢ, summing only within scenario S
3
Total Probability Rule for E(X)
The unconditional expected value equals the probability-weighted sum of all conditional expected values. Conditional and unconditional expectations must be internally consistent — inconsistency creates arbitrage opportunities for other investors.
→ E(X) = E(X|S₁)·P(S₁) + E(X|S₂)·P(S₂) + … + E(X|Sₙ)·P(Sₙ)
4
Conditional Variance
σ²(X | S) measures the dispersion of outcomes within a specific scenario. It uses E(X | S) — not the overall E(X) — as the reference mean. Useful for stress-testing: "What is my risk if the economy enters recession?"
→ σ²(X|S) = Σ P(Xᵢ|S)[Xᵢ − E(X|S)]²
Investment application: Probability trees break a complex forecast (e.g., EPS, operating costs) into stages driven by underlying factors (e.g., interest rate environment, growth rate). Work left to right to assign probabilities at each branch; work right to left to aggregate conditional expected values into a single unconditional forecast at the root.

Section 3 — LO 3Bayes' Formula — Updating Probabilities

1
Prior Probability
The probability of an event based on existing knowledge before receiving new information. Reflects the analyst's current belief or base rate. All prior probabilities across the mutually exclusive and exhaustive event list must sum to 1.
→ Starting point; based on research and historical data
2
Likelihood: P(Information | Event)
The conditional probability of observing the new information, given that a specific event occurred. Must be assessed for every event in the list. This is the key input that reflects how strongly the new information points toward each possible event.
→ "How likely is this announcement if EPS already beat consensus?"
3
Unconditional Probability of the Information: P(Info)
Computed via the total probability rule: P(Info) = Σ P(Info | Eventᵢ) · P(Eventᵢ). This is the normalising denominator in Bayes' formula. It represents the overall chance of observing the new information, averaged across all possible events.
→ Must be calculated before applying Bayes' formula
4
Posterior Probability
The updated probability of the event after incorporating the new information. The ratio P(Info|Event) / P(Info) is the Bayes' multiplier — if greater than 1, the information raises the probability of that event; if less than 1, it lowers it. All posterior probabilities must sum to 1.
→ Posterior = [P(Info|Event) / P(Info)] × Prior
Step-by-step Bayes' process:
  1. List all mutually exclusive and exhaustive events — prior probabilities must sum to 1
  2. Assess the likelihood P(Information | Event) for each event
  3. Compute P(Information) via the total probability rule: Σ P(Info|Eᵢ)·P(Eᵢ)
  4. Apply Bayes' formula to each event: Posterior = [Likelihood / P(Info)] × Prior
  5. Verify: all posterior probabilities must sum to 1

Common PitfallsExam Traps to Avoid

❌ Confusing E(X) with Sample Mean
  • E(X) is a forward-looking probability-weighted forecast (population concept)
  • Sample mean is an equally weighted average of past observations (historical)
  • Do not substitute the sample mean for E(X) in variance calculations
❌ Terminal Node Probability Errors
  • Terminal probability = P(scenario) × P(outcome | scenario)
  • Do not use the conditional probability alone as the terminal probability
  • All terminal node probabilities must sum to 1 — use this as a check
❌ Skipping P(Information) Calculation
  • Bayes' requires P(Information) as the normalising denominator
  • It must be calculated via the total probability rule across all scenarios
  • This is the most commonly missed step in Bayes' exam questions
❌ Non-Exhaustive Event Lists
  • All events must be mutually exclusive AND exhaustive
  • If prior probabilities don't sum to 1, the problem setup is incorrect
  • Posterior probabilities must also sum to 1 — always verify at the end
❌ Reversing P(A|B) and P(B|A)
  • P(EPS beats | expands) ≠ P(expands | EPS beats) — order matters critically
  • Bayes' formula reverses the conditioning: it converts likelihoods into posteriors
  • The entire purpose of Bayes' is this "inverse probability" interpretation
❌ Conditional Variance Uses Wrong Mean
  • σ²(X|S) must use E(X|S) — not overall E(X) — as the deviation reference
  • The unconditional variance is NOT simply the weighted average of conditional variances
  • The full law of total variance is a more advanced topic beyond this reading

Core Mindset for Probability Trees and Conditional Expectations

The two key ideas in this reading are deeply connected. Probability trees are a structured way to compute unconditional expected values by decomposing a problem into conditional scenarios — work left to right to assign probabilities at each branch, then right to left to aggregate conditional expected values into a single unconditional forecast at the root. Bayes' formula is the mathematically rigorous mechanism for updating beliefs when new information arrives: multiply the prior probability by the ratio of how likely the observed information is under that specific event versus overall. The two tools complement each other perfectly — trees visualize the forward-looking structure of a problem, while Bayes' provides the engine for backward inference from observation to cause. On the exam, set up the mutually exclusive and exhaustive event list carefully, verify that prior probabilities sum to 1, compute P(Information) via the total probability rule, apply the formula, and always confirm that posteriors sum to 1 as a final check.

Section 1 — LO 1Portfolio Expected Return

Core formula: E(Rp) = w₁E(R₁) + w₂E(R₂) + … + wₙE(Rₙ) = Σ wᵢE(Rᵢ). Portfolio expected return is simply the weighted average of individual asset expected returns. The weights wᵢ are the proportions of the portfolio invested in each asset and must sum to 1.

Two-Asset Portfolio

E(Rp) = w₁E(R₁) + (1 − w₁)E(R₂). As w₁ increases from 0 to 1, expected return moves linearly between E(R₂) and E(R₁). No need for covariance to compute expected return.

n-Asset Portfolio

E(Rp) = Σ wᵢE(Rᵢ). Weights can be positive (long positions) or negative (short positions). The formula holds exactly regardless of the number of assets or correlations between them.

Section 1 — LO 1Covariance and Correlation

1
Covariance — Cov(Rᵢ, Rⱼ)
The probability-weighted average of cross-products of deviations from expected values. Measures the direction of co-movement between two returns. Can be negative (inverse), zero (unrelated), or positive (moves together). Units are returns squared. Own covariance = variance: Cov(R,R) = σ²(R).
Cov(Rᵢ,Rⱼ) = E[(Rᵢ − E(Rᵢ))(Rⱼ − E(Rⱼ))]
2
Correlation — ρ(Rᵢ, Rⱼ)
Covariance standardised by the product of standard deviations. Ranges from −1 (perfect inverse) to +1 (perfect positive). Scale-free — allows comparison across different asset pairs. Key relationship: Cov(Rᵢ,Rⱼ) = ρ(Rᵢ,Rⱼ) · σ(Rᵢ) · σ(Rⱼ). This rearrangement is essential for computing covariance from correlation inputs.
ρ(Rᵢ,Rⱼ) = Cov(Rᵢ,Rⱼ) / [σ(Rᵢ) · σ(Rⱼ)] → range: [−1, +1]
3
Covariance Matrix
An n × n matrix where diagonal entries are variances (σ²) and off-diagonal entries are covariances. The matrix is symmetric: Cov(Rᵢ,Rⱼ) = Cov(Rⱼ,Rᵢ). For n assets: n variance terms + n(n−1)/2 unique covariances. As n grows, covariance terms vastly outnumber variance terms (e.g., 20 assets → 20 variances but 190 unique covariances).
→ n² total entries; n diagonal (variances); n(n−1)/2 unique off-diagonal covariances
Key insight on diversification: As correlations between assets decrease toward −1, portfolio variance decreases while expected return is unchanged. This is the fundamental benefit of diversification — the same reward at lower risk. When ρ = +1, there is no diversification benefit.

Section 2 — LO 2Covariance via Joint Probability Function

1
Joint Probability Function P(X, Y)
Gives the probability of simultaneously observing specific values of two random variables. For example, P(Rₐ = 25%, R_b = 20%) = 0.20 means there is a 20% probability that asset A returns 25% AND asset B returns 20% in the same period. All joint probabilities must sum to 1.
→ Used when historical data is unavailable and scenarios are modelled directly
2
Computing Covariance from Joint Probabilities
First compute E(Rₐ) and E(R_b) using the marginal probabilities. Then, for each joint outcome, compute the product of deviations from expected values, weight by the joint probability, and sum all terms. This approach is especially useful for stress-testing under discrete scenarios.
Cov(Rₐ,R_b) = Σᵢ Σⱼ P(Rₐᵢ, R_bⱼ) · (Rₐᵢ − E(Rₐ)) · (R_bⱼ − E(R_b))
3
Independent Random Variables
Two variables X and Y are independent if and only if P(X,Y) = P(X) · P(Y) for all values. Independence implies zero correlation, but zero correlation does NOT imply independence (independence is the stronger condition — it rules out all relationships, not just linear ones). For uncorrelated variables: E(XY) = E(X) · E(Y).
→ Independence → Uncorrelated, but Uncorrelated ≠ Independent
🛡️

Section 3 — LO 3Shortfall Risk & Roy's Safety-First Criterion

1
Shortfall Risk
The risk that portfolio return (or value) falls below a minimum acceptable threshold level R_L over some time horizon. Examples include a pension fund's assets falling below liabilities, or a client needing to preserve capital while making a withdrawal. It is a one-sided (downside-only) risk measure.
→ R_L is determined by the client's specific liability or capital preservation need
2
Safety-First Ratio (SFRatio)
Measures how many standard deviations separate the expected portfolio return from the shortfall level. The higher the SFRatio, the lower the probability of falling below R_L (assuming normally distributed returns). When R_L = Rᶠ (risk-free rate), SFRatio = Sharpe Ratio.
SFRatio = [E(Rp) − R_L] / σp
3
Roy's Safety-First Criterion (Optimal Portfolio Selection)
The safety-first optimal portfolio is the one that minimises the probability of falling below R_L, which is equivalent to maximising the SFRatio. Under normality, P(Rp < R_L) = N(−SFRatio). The portfolio with the highest SFRatio has the smallest left-tail probability below R_L.
→ Step 1: Calculate SFRatio for each portfolio. Step 2: Select the portfolio with the highest SFRatio.
Computing shortfall level R_L from a cash need: If a client with a portfolio of value V needs to withdraw amount W at year-end without invading principal, then R_L = W / V. For example, a GBP1,350,000 portfolio needing GBP50,000: R_L = 50,000 / 1,350,000 = 3.7%.

  • For two assets: σ²(Rp) = w₁²σ₁² + w₂²σ₂² + 2w₁w₂Cov(R₁,R₂)

❌ Confusing Covariance and Variance Inputs
  • Covariance matrix diagonal = variances (σ²), not standard deviations
  • To get σ from the diagonal: take the square root of the diagonal entry
  • Off-diagonal entries are already covariances — do not square them
❌ Counting Covariance Terms
  • For n assets: n² total entries, n variances, n(n−1) off-diagonal, n(n−1)/2 unique covariances
  • 5 stocks → 10 unique covariances (NOT 20 or 25)
  • Always divide off-diagonal count by 2 to get unique terms
❌ Safety-First: Wrong R_L or Wrong Sign

❌ Independence vs Uncorrelated

Core Mindset for Portfolio Mathematics

This reading establishes the quantitative foundation of modern portfolio theory. Two fundamental ideas dominate. First, portfolio expected return is simply the weighted average of individual asset expected returns — correlations and covariances play no role here. But portfolio variance is profoundly affected by covariances: as the portfolio grows, the average pairwise covariance between assets becomes the overwhelming driver of portfolio risk, dwarfing individual asset variances. The practical implication is that diversification — adding assets with low or negative correlations — reduces risk without sacrificing expected return. Second, Roy's safety-first criterion translates this framework into a practical decision rule for investors with minimum return constraints: choose the portfolio that maximises the safety-first ratio, which is simply the number of standard deviations between the expected return and the shortfall threshold. When returns are normally distributed, this portfolio minimises the probability of disaster. Recognising that the Sharpe ratio is a special case of the SFRatio (with the risk-free rate as the threshold) connects these two powerful tools.

Section 1 — LO 1The Lognormal Distribution

Definition: A random variable Y is lognormally distributed if its natural logarithm ln(Y) is normally distributed. Conversely, if X is normal and Y = exp(X), then Y is lognormal. Think of it as: "the log is normal."
1
Bounded Below by Zero
Lognormal random variables cannot take negative values. This is critical for modelling asset prices — a stock price can fall toward zero but can never go below it. Normal distributions allow negative values, making them unsuitable as a direct model for prices.
→ P(Y < 0) = 0 for any lognormal variable Y
2
Right-Skewed (Long Right Tail)
Lognormal distributions are skewed to the right. The upside for asset prices is theoretically unlimited (a stock can rise many multiples), but the downside is capped at zero. This asymmetry creates the characteristic long right tail seen in the lognormal distribution and in empirical asset price data.
→ Skewness > 0; mean > median > mode
3
Two Parameters (from the Underlying Normal)
A lognormal distribution is fully described by the mean (μ) and variance (σ²) of its associated normal distribution — these are the parameters of ln(Y), not Y itself. Keep track of both sets: the parameters of the normal (μ, σ²) and the mean and variance of the lognormal variable Y.
→ Mean of Y = exp(μ + 0.5σ²); Variance of Y = exp(2μ + σ²) × [exp(σ²) − 1]
Why is the lognormal mean larger than exp(μ)? As σ² increases, the distribution spreads out. Because it is bounded below by 0, it can only spread upward — pushing the mean to the right. The lognormal mean exp(μ + 0.5σ²) is always greater than exp(μ) by a factor of exp(0.5σ²) > 1.

Section 1 — LO 1Continuously Compounded Returns

Core relationship: If a stock's continuously compounded return r0,T is normally distributed, then the future stock price PT = P0 · exp(r0,T) is lognormally distributed. This is the theoretical foundation for using the lognormal model in finance.

Multi-Period Return is a Sum

The T-period continuously compounded return r0,T equals the sum of one-period returns: r0,T = rT−1,T + rT−2,T−1 + … + r0,1. If one-period returns are i.i.d. normal, their sum is also normal, so r0,T is normal and PT is lognormal.

Mean and Variance Scale Linearly with T

E(r0,T) = μT and σ²(r0,T) = σ²T (from the i.i.d. assumption). Standard deviation scales as σ√T — the square-root-of-time rule. This is foundational in risk management and options pricing.

Central Limit Theorem Backup

Even if individual period returns are not normally distributed, their sum r0,T is approximately normal by the Central Limit Theorem when T is large. This justifies the lognormal model for prices even when returns are not perfectly normal.

Section 1 — LO 1Estimating Volatility from Historical Returns

Annualizing volatility: σ̂annual = σ̂daily × √250. Financial markets typically have ≈250 trading days per year. Volatility is the standard deviation of continuously compounded returns, always quoted on an annualised basis.
1
Step 1 — Compute Daily Continuously Compounded Returns
For each day t, calculate rt = ln(Pt / Pt−1). This converts holding-period price changes into continuously compounded daily returns. Use natural logarithm (ln), not log base 10.
→ rt = ln(Pt / Pt−1)
2
Step 2 — Compute Sample Standard Deviation
Calculate the mean of the daily returns, then the sample standard deviation using divisor (n − 1). This is σ̂daily, the sample estimate of one-period volatility.
→ σ̂daily = sample std dev of {r1, r2, …, rn}
3
Step 3 — Annualise Using the Square-Root Rule
Multiply daily volatility by √250 to obtain annualised volatility. For example, if σ̂daily = 0.021261, then annualised volatility = 0.021261 × √250 = 0.3362 = 33.6%. This follows directly from σ²(r0,T) = σ²T.
→ σ̂annual = σ̂daily × √250 ≈ σ̂daily × 15.81
Key exam note: Always annualise based on 250 trading days, not 365 calendar days. The mean continuously compounded annual return is estimated as μ̂ × 250 (where μ̂ is the sample mean of daily returns), but with only a few days of data this estimate is extremely unreliable — the variance in daily returns overwhelms the signal in expected return.

Section 2 — LO 2Monte Carlo Simulation

Definition: Monte Carlo simulation generates a large number of random samples from a specified probability distribution (or distributions) to obtain the likelihood of a range of results. It is used when analytical solutions are unavailable or impractical.

Investment Risk & Return Estimation

Simulate portfolio profit and loss over a specified horizon — either asset-by-asset or at the aggregate portfolio level. Repeated trials produce a simulated frequency distribution of returns from which performance and risk measures (VaR, CVaR, expected return) are derived.

Valuing Complex Securities

Monte Carlo is particularly valuable for securities with complex embedded options (e.g., mortgage-backed securities, Asian options, lookback options) where no closed-form analytical pricing formula exists. The analyst controls assumptions and can test model sensitivity.

Sensitivity & Scenario Analysis

Because the analyst specifies all inputs, Monte Carlo is ideal for "what-if" analysis — examining how changes in key distributional assumptions (mean, volatility, correlation) affect outcomes. This makes it a powerful complement to analytical models.

⚙️

Section 2 — LO 2Six Steps of Monte Carlo Simulation

1
Specify the Quantity of Interest
Define what you are trying to value or estimate (e.g., value of a contingent claim CiT) and identify the underlying variables (e.g., stock price). Specify the starting values of all underlying variables.
→ e.g., Asian option value = max(Final Price − Average Price, 0)
2
Specify a Time Grid
Divide the total horizon into K sub-periods. The time increment Δt = Calendar time / K. For a 1-year option simulated monthly: K = 12, Δt = 1 month. The granularity of the time grid affects accuracy and computation time.
→ Δt = T / K; e.g., 1 year ÷ 12 months = 1/12 year per step
3
Specify Distributional Assumptions & Draw Random Numbers
Choose a model for the key risk factor and its distribution. For stock prices, a standard model is: ΔStock price = (μ × Prior price × Δt) + (σ × Prior price × Zk), where Zk ~ N(0,1). Draw K random values of Zk per simulation trial.
→ ΔS = μS·Δt + σS·Zk; draw K standard normal values per trial
4
Convert Random Numbers to Asset Price Path
Use the drawn random numbers and the stock price model to generate a sequence of K stock prices for the trial. Starting from P0, apply ΔStock price at each sub-period to build the full price path.
→ Produces one simulated price path: P0, P1, …, PK
5
Calculate the Payoff for This Trial
For each simulated price path, compute the average stock price and the value of the contingent claim at maturity CiT. Then discount to t = 0 to get Ci0. This completes one simulation trial (one iteration).
→ CiT = max(PK − avg(P1…PK), 0); Ci0 = PV(CiT)
6
Repeat & Compute Summary Statistics
Repeat Steps 4–5 for the required number of trials (e.g., I = 1,000). The Monte Carlo estimate of the security's value is the mean of all Ci0 values across all trials. Other statistics (std dev, percentiles) characterise the distribution of outcomes.
→ Monte Carlo value = (1/I) × ΣCi0 over all I trials

Section 3 — LO 3Bootstrap Resampling

Core idea: Bootstrap mimics the process of drawing random samples from a population by instead sampling (with replacement) from the observed sample, treating it as a proxy for the population. It builds a sampling distribution empirically — no distributional assumptions required.
1
Sampling With Replacement
From an original sample of size n, repeatedly draw a new resample of size n. Crucially, each item drawn is returned before the next draw — so the same observation can appear multiple times in one resample, and some observations may not appear at all. This is the defining feature of bootstrapping.
→ Each bootstrap resample has the same size n as the original sample
2
Empirical Distribution as Population Proxy
Instead of specifying a theoretical distribution (as in Monte Carlo), bootstrap uses the observed historical data (e.g., past stock returns) as the empirical distribution. The quality of bootstrap inference depends on how representative the original sample is of the true population.
→ No need to assume normality or any other parametric form
3
Constructing the Bootstrap Sampling Distribution
For each of B resamples, calculate the statistic of interest (mean, variance, skewness, etc.). The distribution of these B estimates forms the bootstrap sampling distribution, which approximates the true sampling distribution. Summary statistics of this distribution (mean, standard error, confidence intervals) are the bootstrap estimates.
→ B bootstrap estimates → sampling distribution → population inference

Section 1 — LO 1Sampling from Different Distributions

Key principle: Samples must be drawn from a single, homogeneous population. Combining data from two different distributions (e.g., different investment strategies across time) creates a sample that represents no actual population — any conclusions drawn are invalid.

The Sharpe Ratio Trap

If a manager uses a low-risk strategy in Year 1 and a high-risk strategy in Year 2, pooling eight quarters of returns mixes two different distributions. The combined Sharpe ratio will differ from — and can be lower than — the separate yearly Sharpe ratios, producing misleading conclusions about performance.

Larger is Not Always Better

A larger sample is only superior when it remains representative of a single homogeneous population. When the underlying distribution changes over time (e.g., regime change, strategy shift), a smaller, more recent sample may be more informative than a larger pooled one.

Section 2 — LO 2The Central Limit Theorem (CLT)

Formal statement: Given a population with mean μ and finite variance σ², the sampling distribution of the sample mean X̄, computed from random samples of size n, will be approximately normal with mean μ and variance σ²/n when sample size n is large (generally n ≥ 30).
1
Property 1 — Approximate Normality
The sampling distribution of X̄ will be approximately normally distributed regardless of the shape of the underlying population distribution, provided the population has finite variance and the sample size is large (n ≥ 30 as a rule of thumb). Highly non-normal populations may require n well above 30.
→ CLT holds for any distribution with finite variance — normal, skewed, uniform, etc.
2
Property 2 — Mean of X̄ Equals Population Mean
The mean of the sampling distribution of X̄ equals the population mean μ. The sample mean is an unbiased estimator of the population mean — on average across many samples, it hits the target.
→ E(X̄) = μ
3
Property 3 — Variance of X̄ Equals σ²/n
The variance of the sampling distribution is the population variance divided by sample size. As n increases, σ²/n decreases — the distribution of X̄ concentrates more tightly around μ. This is why larger samples give more precise estimates of the population mean.
→ Var(X̄) = σ²/n; as n → ∞, Var(X̄) → 0
Why the CLT matters: It allows analysts to make precise probability statements about the population mean using the sample mean — and to construct confidence intervals and conduct hypothesis tests — even when the population distribution is unknown, as long as the sample is large enough.

Standard Deviation vs. Standard Error — Key Distinction

Standard deviation measures the dispersion of individual data points around the mean — it describes the spread of the data. Standard error measures the precision of the sample mean as an estimate of the population mean — it describes inferential accuracy. They are not interchangeable concepts.

Effect of Sample Size on Standard Error

Standard error falls with √n: doubling the sample size reduces standard error by a factor of √2 ≈ 1.41. To halve the standard error, you must quadruple the sample size. This diminishing return means there is a point beyond which adding more observations yields little additional precision.

Section 3 — LO 3Resampling: Bootstrap and Jackknife

What is resampling? Resampling repeatedly draws samples from the original observed data to perform statistical inference about population parameters — without relying on analytical formulas (z-statistics, t-statistics). It is particularly powerful when no closed-form formula exists for the statistic of interest (e.g., the median).
1
Bootstrap — With Replacement
Draw B resamples from the original sample, each of size n, with replacement. The same observation can appear multiple times in one resample; some observations may not appear at all. Compute the statistic of interest (e.g., mean, median) for each resample. The distribution of these B estimates approximates the true sampling distribution. Often called model-free or non-parametric resampling.
→ Each resample has same size n as original; with replacement; B determined by analyst
2
Jackknife — Leave-One-Out
For a sample of size n, create n sub-samples, each formed by leaving out one different observation at a time (without replacement). Compute the statistic for each sub-sample. Jackknife is deterministic — it always produces the same results for the same dataset. Primarily used to reduce estimator bias and to estimate standard errors and confidence intervals.
→ Exactly n repetitions for sample of size n; without replacement; deterministic

Section 3 — LO 3Bootstrap Standard Error — Step by Step

1
Draw B resamples with replacement
From the original sample of size n, draw B random samples (each of size n) with replacement. B is chosen by the analyst — larger B gives a more stable estimate of the sampling distribution. Typical values range from 500 to 10,000+.
→ Each resample is independently drawn; same n as original
2
Compute the statistic for each resample
Calculate the statistic of interest (e.g., mean θ̂b) for each of the B resamples. These B values form the bootstrap sampling distribution — the empirical approximation of the true sampling distribution of the statistic.
→ Obtain θ̂1, θ̂2, …, θ̂B
3
Compute the mean of all resample statistics
Calculate θ̄ = (1/B) × Σθ̂b — the mean across all B resample means. This is the centre of the bootstrap sampling distribution.
→ θ̄ = average of all B resample statistics
4
Calculate the bootstrap standard error
Apply the formula: sX̄ = √[ (1/(B−1)) · Σ(θ̂b − θ̄)² ]. This is the standard deviation of the bootstrap sampling distribution, which approximates the standard error of the statistic. Note the divisor is B − 1, not B (analogous to sample standard deviation).
→ sX̄ = √[ (1/(B−1)) · Σ(θ̂b − θ̄)² ]
Bootstrap advantage: The formula s / √n only works for the sample mean. Bootstrap works for any statistic — including the median, Sharpe ratio, or other complex estimators — without requiring an analytical formula.

Core Mindset for Estimation and Inference

This reading builds the statistical bridge between samples and populations. The key insight is that every sample statistic is itself a random variable with a probability distribution — the sampling distribution. The central limit theorem is the engine that makes inference tractable: regardless of how the underlying population is distributed (so long as it has finite variance), the distribution of the sample mean becomes approximately normal as sample size grows, with mean equal to the population mean and variance equal to σ²/n. This single result underpins confidence intervals and hypothesis testing. Sampling method choice matters too: stratified sampling improves precision over simple random by ensuring all subgroups are represented — critical in fixed-income indexing. And when no analytical formula exists for a statistic's standard error, bootstrap resampling provides a model-free solution that works for any estimator, from the mean to the median to the Sharpe ratio.

State both H₀ (null) and Hᵃ (alternative) in terms of population parameters. H₀ is what we want to reject; Hᵃ is the "suspected" or "hoped for" condition. Both must be mutually exclusive and collectively exhaustive. H₀ always includes an equality sign (=, ≤, or ≥).

2
Identify the Appropriate Test Statistic
Choose the test statistic and its probability distribution based on what is being tested (mean, variance, correlation) and available information (known vs. unknown population variance). The distribution drives the critical values used in Step 4.
→ See the master reference table below
3
Specify the Level of Significance
Choose α — the probability of a Type I error. Common values: 1%, 5%, 10%. Confidence level = 1 − α. A smaller α reduces Type I error probability but increases Type II error probability. The only way to reduce both simultaneously is to increase sample size n.
→ α = 5% corresponds to a 95% confidence level
4
State the Decision Rule
Determine critical value(s) from α and the test distribution. Reject H₀ if the calculated statistic exceeds the critical value(s). P-value approach: reject H₀ if p-value < α. The p-value is the smallest α at which H₀ can be rejected.
→ One-tailed: one critical value. Two-tailed: two critical values (±)
5
Collect Data and Calculate the Test Statistic
Use sample data to compute the numerical value of the test statistic using the appropriate formula from Step 2.
→ Compared to critical values in Step 6
6
Make a Decision
If calculated statistic falls in the rejection region → reject H₀ (statistically significant). Otherwise → fail to reject H₀. Note: we never "accept" H₀. A confidence interval approach also works: if the hypothesised value falls within the interval, fail to reject H₀.
→ "Fail to reject" does not mean H₀ is proven true

Section 2 — LO 2Key Tests in Detail

t-Test for a Single Mean

Assumptions: approximately normal population, unknown variance (the standard case in practice). Formula: t = (X̄ − μ₀) / (s/√n), df = n − 1. Two-tailed: reject if |t| > t_critical. Confidence interval alternative: reject H₀ if μ₀ falls outside X̄ ± t_crit × (s/√n).

Chi-Square Test for Single Variance

Assumptions: normally distributed population. Formula: χ² = (n−1)s² / σ₀², df = n − 1. Chi-square distribution is always positive and asymmetric. For left-tailed tests (Hᵃ: σ² < σ₀²), reject H₀ if χ² falls to the left of the lower critical value (e.g., 13.09 for df = 23 at α = 5%).

F-Test for Equality of Two Variances

Assumptions: two independent random samples from normally distributed populations. Place the larger sample variance in the numerator. F is always positive. Two-tailed: use α/2 critical values at each tail. Reject H₀ if F > upper critical value or F < lower critical value. Can also compute as the reciprocal and compare to the same boundaries.

One-Tailed vs. Two-Tailed Tests

Two-tailed (≠): critical region split equally across both tails; absolute critical value is larger — harder to reject in any specific direction. One-tailed (> or <): entire rejection region in one tail; smaller absolute critical value — easier to reject in the hypothesised direction. One-tailed tests more strongly reflect the analyst's directional belief.

Nonparametric alternative to the t-test for a single mean and for paired comparisons. Works on ranked absolute differences. Use when the population is non-normal or the sample is small.

Nonparametric alternative for testing the difference between two independent group means (also known as Wilcoxon rank sum test). Use when normality fails or samples are small and non-normal.

Core Mindset for Hypothesis Testing

Hypothesis testing is the bridge between data and decisions. The six-step process is universal — apply it consistently to any test and it will never lead you astray. The two error types reflect an unavoidable trade-off: reducing a false positive always increases the risk of a false negative. Power — catching a real effect when it exists — is the goal, and it rises with sample size, effect magnitude, and a higher α. Match your test statistic to the question: t for means, chi-square for a single variance, F for comparing two variances. And when the parametric assumptions break down — small samples, ranked data, non-normal populations, outliers — don't force it: use nonparametric alternatives designed for exactly those situations.

Section 1 — LO 1Parametric Test: Pearson Correlation

Formula: rXY = sXY / (sX · sY)  ·  Test statistic: t = r√(n − 2) / √(1 − r²)  ·  Distribution: t with n − 2 degrees of freedom

Hypotheses

Two-sided: H₀: ρ = 0 vs. Hᵃ: ρ ≠ 0  |  One-sided right: H₀: ρ ≤ 0 vs. Hᵃ: ρ > 0  |  One-sided left: H₀: ρ ≥ 0 vs. Hᵃ: ρ < 0. The most common test asks whether any relationship exists at all, which implies H₀: ρ = 0.

Effect of Sample Size on Significance

As n increases, the same sample correlation r becomes easier to reject. Two reasons: (1) degrees of freedom increase so the critical t-value decreases; (2) the numerator r√(n−2) grows. E.g., r = 0.35 with n = 12 gives t = 1.182 (not significant at 5%); with n = 32 gives t = 2.046 (just significant). As datasets become ever larger, nearly any correlation becomes statistically significant — other analytical tools must be applied.

Assumptions

The two variables must be normally distributed (parametric assumption). The correlation measures the linear relationship only. The sign of the correlation is driven by the covariance; each observation is compared to its variable mean.

🔢

Section 1 — LO 1Nonparametric Test: Spearman Rank Correlation

Formula: rS = 1 − 6Σd²i / [n(n² − 1)]  ·  Same t-test statistic applies for n > 30: t = rS√(n − 2) / √(1 − rS²), df = n − 2
1
Rank Each Variable
Rank observations on X from largest (rank 1) to smallest. Repeat for Y. For tied values, assign the average of the tied ranks (e.g., if 3rd and 4th values are tied, both get rank 3.5).
→ Produces two sets of ranks, one per variable
2
Calculate Rank Differences
For each observation i, compute di = Rank(Xi) − Rank(Yi), then di². Sum all di² to get Σd².
→ Σd² captures the total discrepancy in rankings between the two variables
3
Apply the Formula
rS = 1 − 6Σd² / [n(n² − 1)]. This is the Pearson correlation applied to ranks rather than raw values. For the large-sample case (n > 30), test significance using the same t-statistic as for Pearson.
→ Small samples (n ≤ 30) require a specialised critical value table

When to Use Spearman

Use when the population departs meaningfully from normality, when there are outliers, when data are ranked/ordinal, or when assumptions for the Pearson parametric test are not met.

Spearman vs. Pearson

Spearman is calculated on ranks, not raw values. It is less sensitive to outliers. When normality holds, Pearson is preferred because it has greater statistical power.

Large Sample (n > 30)

Use the same t-test as for Pearson (df = n − 2). The test statistic formula is identical: t = rS√(n − 2) / √(1 − rS²).

Small Sample (n ≤ 30)

Require a specialised critical value table specific to the Spearman rank correlation. The standard t-distribution critical values are not appropriate.

Section 2 — LO 2Tests of Independence: Contingency Tables

Chi-square statistic: χ² = Σ (Oij − Eij)² / Eij  ·  Expected frequency: Eij = (Row i total × Column j total) / Overall total  ·  df = (r − 1)(c − 1)

When to Use a Contingency Table Test

Use when data are categorical or discrete — correlation methods cannot be applied. A contingency table (two-way table) cross-classifies observations on two categorical dimensions. Example: 1,594 ETFs classified by size (small/medium/large) AND investment type (value/growth/blend).

Hypotheses

H₀: The two categorical variables are independent (no relationship). Hᵃ: The two categorical variables are dependent (a relationship exists). This is always a one-sided (right-tail only) test because the chi-square statistic is always non-negative — observed equals expected gives χ² = 0; any deviation gives χ² > 0.

Degrees of Freedom

df = (r − 1)(c − 1), where r = number of rows (categories of variable 1) and c = number of columns (categories of variable 2). Example: 3 × 3 table → df = (3−1)(3−1) = 4. Critical value at 5% significance with df = 4 is 9.4877.

Decision Rule

Reject H₀ if calculated χ² > critical χ². There is only one rejection region (right tail). The test is nonparametric — it makes no distributional assumptions about the underlying populations.

🔄

Section 2 — LO 2Chi-Square Test: Step-by-Step

1
State the Hypotheses
H₀: The two classifications are independent. Hᵃ: The two classifications are not independent (dependent).
→ Independence means knowing one category tells you nothing about the other
2
Calculate Expected Frequencies
For each cell (i, j): Eij = (Row i total × Column j total) / Overall total. This is the count you would expect in that cell if the two variables were completely independent.
→ Eij = (Row total × Column total) / Grand total
3
Calculate Scaled Squared Deviations
For each cell: (Oij − Eij)² / Eij. This scales each squared difference by the expected count — cells with higher expected counts get proportionally less weight per unit deviation.
→ Each cell contributes one term to the sum
4
Sum to Get χ²
χ² = sum of all (Oij − Eij)² / Eij across all m cells, where m = r × c. Compare to the chi-square critical value with df = (r−1)(c−1) at the chosen significance level.
→ Reject H₀ if χ² > critical value (right-tail only)

Dependent Variable (Y)

The variable whose variation we are trying to explain. Plotted on the vertical axis. Examples: ROA, stock return, revenue growth, inflation rate.

Independent Variable (X)

The variable used to explain variation in Y. Plotted on the horizontal axis. Must not be random (non-stochastic assumption). Examples: CAPEX, GDP growth, market return.

Value of Y when X = 0. Calculated as: b̂0 = Ȳ − b̂1X̄. May be meaningless if X = 0 is unrealistic (e.g., zero money supply). In indicator variable regressions, it is the mean of Y when the indicator = 0.

Change in Y for a one-unit change in X. Formula: b̂1 = Σ(Yi−Ȳ)(Xi−X̄) / Σ(Xi−X̄)² = Cov(X,Y) / Var(X). Same sign as the pairwise correlation. In indicator variable regressions, it is the difference in group means.

Ordinary least squares minimises the sum of squared residuals (SSE) = Σei². The resulting line is the best-fitting line through the data. By construction: (1) sum of residuals = 0; (2) regression line passes through (X̄, Ȳ).

Slope vs. Correlation: Both share the same sign and same numerator Σ(Yi−Ȳ)(Xi−X̄). The difference is the denominator — slope uses Var(X) only; correlation uses Std(Y) × Std(X). In SLR: r = b̂1 × (sX/sY).

Section 3 — LO 3Hypothesis Tests on Regression Coefficients

Test of the Slope (t-test)

H0: b1 = B1 (often 0) vs. Ha: b1 ≠ B1. Formula: t = (b̂1 − B1) / sb̂₁, where sb̂₁ = se / √Σ(Xi−X̄)². df = n − 2. Greater variability in X → smaller sb̂₁ → larger |t|. In SLR, the t-test for b1 = 0 and the t-test for ρ = 0 produce the same statistic.

Test of the Intercept (t-test)

H0: b0 = B0 vs. Ha: b0 ≠ B0. Formula: t = (b̂0 − B0) / sb̂₀, where sb̂₀ = se√[1/n + X̄² / Σ(Xi−X̄)²]. df = n − 2. Intercept test is meaningful only if X = 0 is economically sensible.

Indicator (Dummy) Variables

X takes only values 0 or 1 (e.g., earnings announcement month = 1, otherwise = 0). Intercept = mean of Y when indicator = 0. Slope = difference in means between the two groups. The test of the slope coefficient is equivalent to a two-sample t-test of means.

p-Values

The p-value is the smallest α at which H0 can be rejected. Reject H0 if p-value < α. Most software reports p-values for the test H0: parameter = 0. For a one-sided test, divide the two-sided p-value by 2. A very small p-value does not imply economic significance — large samples can make trivially small effects significant.

Section 5 — LO 5Prediction & Prediction Intervals

Point forecast: Ŷf = b̂0 + b̂1Xf  ·  Prediction interval: Ŷf ± tα/2 × sf  ·  Standard error of forecast: sf = se√[1 + 1/n + (Xf−X̄)²/Σ(Xi−X̄)²]
1
Make the Point Forecast
Ŷf = b̂0 + b̂1Xf. Plug the forecasted value of X into the estimated regression equation.
→ This is the estimated mean of Y at X = Xf
2
Compute sf
sf = se√[1 + 1/n + (Xf−X̄)²/Σ(Xi−X̄)²]. Three factors increase sf: (1) larger se; (2) smaller n; (3) Xf further from X̄. The interval is narrowest at Xf = X̄ and widens as you move away.
→ Forecasting outside the data range (extrapolation) increases uncertainty dramatically
3
Apply the Interval
Prediction interval = Ŷf ± tcritical α/2 × sf, with df = n − 2. This interval accounts for both estimation error in the regression line AND individual observation scatter around the line (hence the "+1" inside the square root).
→ Wider than a confidence interval for the mean — it must capture individual Y values
Important: The "1 +" in the sf formula captures the fact that we're predicting an individual Y value, not the conditional mean of Y. This makes prediction intervals wider than confidence intervals for the mean.

Goal: Transform non-linear data so that the linear regression model fits well. Choose based on R², F-statistic, se, and random (pattern-free) residuals.

📊 Log-Lin

ln Y = b0 + b1X. Used for exponentially growing variables (e.g., revenues growing at a compound rate). To forecast Y: antilog the predicted ln Y — i.e., Y = epredicted ln Y. Cannot compare R² directly to a lin-lin model.

📉 Lin-Log

Y = b0 + b1ln X. Used when X has diminishing marginal impact on Y (e.g., operating margin vs. unit sales). Since Y is unchanged, R² and se can be compared directly with the lin-lin model.

🔢 Log-Log

ln Y = b0 + b1ln X. The slope is a constant elasticity: 1% change in X → b1% change in Y. Used for revenue–advertising, demand models. Cannot compare R² directly to lin-lin.

🎯 Model Selection

Compare models with the same dependent variable form using R², F, and se. Always examine residuals: random residuals confirm correct functional form. Non-random residuals signal the wrong form.

Common PitfallsExam Traps to Avoid

❌ Error vs. Residual
  • Error term (ε) = population; Residual (e) = sample estimate
  • Sum of residuals is always zero by construction in OLS
  • Residuals are in the same units as Y (not standardised)
❌ Normality Assumption
  • Normality applies to the RESIDUALS, not to Y or X themselves
  • Outliers in Y or X can severely distort the fitted line
  • Large n → CLT allows relaxing normality requirement
❌ R² vs. F vs. se
  • R² is descriptive (relative); F is a statistical test (relative); se is absolute (has units)
  • Only compare R² and F across models with the SAME dependent variable form
  • Can always compare se if dependent variable is the same
❌ Prediction Interval Width
  • Narrowest at Xf = X̄; wider as Xf deviates from X̄
  • Extrapolating outside the data range → unreliable wide intervals
  • Larger n reduces sf; better model fit (lower se) also reduces sf
❌ Indicator Variable Interpretation
  • Intercept = mean of Y when indicator = 0 (the reference group)
  • Slope = DIFFERENCE in means between the two groups (not the mean of group 1)
  • t-test on slope ≡ two-sample pooled-variance t-test
❌ Log Model Forecasting
  • Log-lin forecast: predicted Y = epredicted ln Y (take antilog)
  • Cannot compare R² between log-lin and lin-lin (different dependent variable)
  • Log-log slope = elasticity: % change in Y per % change in X

Core Mindset for Simple Linear Regression

Simple linear regression is the CFA's primary tool for quantifying relationships and making predictions. Master four things: (1) OLS mechanics — slope = Cov(Y,X)/Var(X), intercept = Ȳ − b̂1X̄, and residuals sum to zero; (2) the LINE assumptions — violation patterns show up in residual plots; (3) the ANOVA decomposition — SST = SSR + SSE drives R², F, and se, with t² = F in SLR; and (4) the prediction interval — it's wider than a confidence interval, narrowest at X̄, and widens with extrapolation. For functional forms, always ask: does transforming the variable improve the residual plot and the fit statistics? And remember — statistical significance ≠ economic significance, especially with large samples.

Structured Data

Organised in tables; each field represents the same type of information. Stored in traditional relational databases (SQL). Examples: stock prices, corporate financials, economic data, trade prices and volumes.

Unstructured Data

Disparate, unorganised data that cannot be represented in tabular form. Requires specialised applications before it is usable. Examples: social media posts, emails, text messages, voice recordings, images, video, satellite feeds, blog content.

Semi-Structured Data

Has attributes of both structured and unstructured data. Partially organised but not fully conforming to a relational model. Examples: HTML code, XML, JSON files, emails with structured metadata but unstructured body text.

Veracity: The Critical Fourth V

Becomes especially important with Big Data because of the enormous variety of sources and the risk of mixing high-quality and low-quality data. Big Data amplifies the age-old challenge of disentangling quality from quantity. Requires data curation and cleaning before analysis.

Section 2 — LO 2Artificial Intelligence & Machine Learning

AI: Computer systems capable of performing tasks that traditionally required human intelligence at comparable or superior levels.  ·  ML: Computer-based techniques that extract knowledge from large data without assumptions about underlying probability distributions. Goal: "find the pattern, apply the pattern."

Expert Systems (Early AI)

Simulated human expert knowledge through "if–then" rule sets. Deterministic, not learning-based. Limited to pre-programmed knowledge. Used in early fraud detection and decision support systems.

ML: The Three-Dataset Split

Training set — algorithm identifies input–output relationships from historical data. Validation set — model is refined and tuned. Test set — model's predictive power on entirely new data is evaluated. Must pass all three stages before deployment.

Overfitting

Model learns the training data too precisely — treats noise as true signal. Result: performs well on training data but fails on new data. Indicates a model that is too complex. The primary risk in ML investment models.

Underfitting

Model treats true signal as noise — fails to recognise genuine patterns in the training data. Result: poor performance even on training data. Indicates a model that is too simplistic.

"Black Box" Problem

Because ML techniques are not explicitly programmed, the process by which they arrive at outcomes may be opaque and difficult to explain or interpret. A significant concern for compliance, regulatory oversight, and investment attribution.

Human Judgment Still Required

Despite automation, ML still requires human expertise to: understand the underlying data, select appropriate techniques, clean data of biases and spurious points, and ensure sufficient data volume exists for training and validation.

🔀

Section 2 — LO 2Three Types of Machine Learning

1
Supervised Learning
Inputs AND outputs are labelled (identified) for the algorithm. The algorithm learns how to map inputs to outputs using historical labelled data, then applies that mapping to new datasets. Requires labelled training data — the most data-intensive category.
→ Examples: predicting stock returns from financial variables; classifying market days as up/flat/down; credit scoring
2
Unsupervised Learning
Only inputs (data) are provided — no labelled outputs. The algorithm identifies the underlying structure, clusters, or patterns within the data entirely on its own. Does not predict a specific output; instead, discovers hidden organisation.
→ Examples: grouping companies into peer groups based on characteristics (not standard sectors); customer segmentation; anomaly detection
3
Deep Learning (Deep Neural Networks)
Uses neural networks with many hidden layers to perform multistage, non-linear data processing. Can use supervised or unsupervised approaches. Develops understanding of simple concepts that informs analysis of more complex ones — "layered" intelligence. Neural networks have existed since 1958; modern deep learning algorithms require less computing power and achieve far better performance.
→ Examples: image recognition (satellite parking lot counts), speech recognition, pattern recognition in market data
Why now? Big Data solved the historic data shortage that constrained ML. Growth in Big Data provided the training volumes needed for neural networks to model relationships with sufficient accuracy. Combined with falling storage costs and faster processors, this unlocked broad ML deployment.

Section 3 — LO 3Data Science: Processing & Visualisation

Data Science: Interdisciplinary field harnessing computer science (including ML), statistics, and other disciplines to extract information from Big Data for business and investment purposes.

📥 Capture

How data are collected and transformed into a usable format. Low-latency systems communicate high volumes of data with minimal delay — essential for automated/algorithmic trading. High-latency systems are acceptable for non-real-time research.

🧹 Curation

The data cleaning exercise: reviewing all data to detect errors, bad or inaccurate data, and making adjustments for missing data. Critical first step — output quality is bounded by input data quality. Particularly difficult with unstructured alternative data.

🗄️ Storage

How data are recorded, archived, and accessed. Key considerations: (1) structured vs. unstructured data; (2) whether analytical needs require low-latency solutions. Database type must match data structure (SQL for structured; NoSQL for unstructured).

🔍 Search

How to query and locate specific data within very large datasets. Big Data requires advanced query applications capable of rapidly scanning massive stores — standard database queries become insufficient at Big Data scale.

🔄 Transfer

How data move from source or storage to the analytical tool. Mechanisms include direct data feeds (e.g., exchange price feeds), APIs, and batch transfers. Latency requirements dictate transfer method.

📊 Visualisation

Formatting and displaying data graphically to identify trends and relationships. Traditional: tables, charts. For Big Data: interactive 3D graphics, heat maps, tree diagrams, network graphs, tag clouds (word frequency), and mind maps (concept relationships).

Section 3 — LO 3Text Analytics & Natural Language Processing (NLP)

Text Analytics

Computer programs that analyse and derive meaning from large, unstructured text or voice-based datasets — company filings, earnings calls, social media, email, internet postings, surveys. Includes: automated information retrieval, lexical analysis (word frequency), pattern recognition (key words and phrases), and predictive analysis (identifying leading indicators).

NLP: Natural Language Processing

A specific field within text analytics at the intersection of computer science, AI, and linguistics. Focuses on developing programs to analyse and interpret human language. Automated NLP tasks include: translation, speech recognition, text mining, sentiment analysis, and topic analysis. Used in compliance to review communications for adherence to policy, fraud detection, and confidentiality monitoring.

Sentiment Analysis in Investment Practice

NLP assigns sentiment ratings (very negative → very positive) to analyst commentary, earnings call transcripts, and news articles. Detects and monitors shifts in sentiment ahead of explicit recommendation changes — a leading signal. Scales across thousands of companies worldwide, performing work previously done by human analysts.

Central Bank & Policy Maker Analysis

NLP analyses transcripts from central banks (Fed, ECB) for subtle signals in topic selection, word choice, and inferred tone — covering interest rate policy, output, and inflation expectations. Identifies trending or waning topics of institutional focus before they are made explicit.

NLP Application: IPO and Stock Returns

Past research has evaluated the predictive power of Twitter sentiment regarding IPO performance and the effect of positive/negative news sentiment on stock returns. Models incorporating NLP provide non-traditional information about consumer preferences, opinions, and behaviours to forecast near-term trends.

  • Deep learning can use EITHER supervised OR unsupervised approaches
❌ Overfitting vs. Underfitting
  • Overfitting = too complex; learns noise as signal; fails on new data
  • Underfitting = too simple; misses true patterns even in training data
  • Both produce bad predictions — validation and test sets are designed to catch both
❌ Corporate Exhaust Timing
  • Business process data (including corporate exhaust) can be LEADING or real-time indicators
  • Traditional corporate metrics (earnings, financials) are LAGGING — reported quarterly or annually
  • This timing advantage is the key investment value of alternative data

  • NLP = specific application of text analytics using linguistic insight into human language structure
  • NLP is a subset of text analytics, not a synonym

Core Mindset for Big Data & Fintech

This reading is fundamentally about how modern technology has transformed the data available to investors and the tools used to analyse it. The four Vs frame what Big Data is — but veracity is the examiner's favourite because it separates the "gathering" Vs (volume, velocity, variety) from the "quality" concern that matters most for investment decisions. Machine learning has three types: supervised (labelled input–output, prediction), unsupervised (no labels, structure discovery), and deep learning (neural networks, layered non-linear processing). The two failure modes — overfitting (model too complex, memorises noise) and underfitting (model too simple, misses signal) — are tested directly. For applications, the key insight is that alternative data — particularly from individuals, business processes, and sensors — provides leading indicators that traditional lagging financial data cannot. NLP operationalises this by converting unstructured text into investment signals at machine scale.
